LUKE 1:57-66 - PERICOPE PODCAST (English)
Title: "His Name Is John"
Duration: ~10 minutes
Hosts: Samuel and Maya

---

SAMUEL: We're back in Luke chapter one, verses fifty-seven through sixty-six. Elizabeth gives birth, and what should be a simple naming ceremony becomes a prophetic showdown.

MAYA: Let's set the scene. "When the time came for Elizabeth to have her baby, she gave birth to a son. Her neighbors and relatives heard that the Lord had shown her great mercy, and they celebrated with her."

SAMUEL: In that culture, childbirth - especially to a woman who'd been considered barren - was a community event. Neighbors and family would gather. There would be celebration. And on the eighth day, they'd come for the circumcision.

MAYA: Circumcision was performed on day eight as commanded in Leviticus, even if that day fell on the Sabbath. It was the covenant sign given to Abraham - the mark of belonging to Israel.

SAMUEL: In this period, the naming often happened at circumcision. The community participated. It was social, not just religious.

MAYA: And the assumption was clear. Verse fifty-nine: "They were going to name him Zechariah after his father."

SAMUEL: This was standard practice - naming children after fathers or grandfathers. Continuing the family line. Honoring ancestors. No one expected anything different.

MAYA: But then Elizabeth speaks up. "No! He is to be called John."

SAMUEL: That's emphatic in Greek. Ouchi - "No way!" This elderly woman is publicly contradicting the community's assumption. Socially bold for anyone, especially a woman in that context.

MAYA: The neighbors push back. "None of your relatives has that name." Family names mattered. A completely new name - John - was unexpected and unexplained.

SAMUEL: So they turn to Zechariah. Remember, he's been mute for nine months. Since the angel appeared in the temple. He can't speak.

MAYA: "They made signs to his father, asking what he wanted to name the child." This suggests Zechariah might also have been deaf - otherwise why sign to him instead of simply asking?

SAMUEL: He asks for a writing tablet - a small wooden board covered with wax that you'd scratch letters into. And he writes: "His name is John."

MAYA: Not "I want to name him John" or "Let's call him John." "His name IS John." It's already decided. The angel named him months ago. Zechariah is simply confirming what God determined.

SAMUEL: And immediately - parachrona in Greek, meaning "at that very moment" - his mouth is opened and his tongue released. His first words after nine months of silence are praise.

MAYA: That detail matters. Zechariah's silence began as a consequence of doubt. It ends with worship. His first words aren't complaint or explanation - they're eulogon, blessing God.

SAMUEL: Nine months of silence can change a person. We might imagine him processing everything that happened. The angel. The promises. His own doubt. His wife's pregnancy. All those months of being unable to speak.

MAYA: When he finally opens his mouth, what comes out is pure praise. The enforced silence seems to have been formative, not just punitive.

SAMUEL: The community's response: "Fear came on all their neighbors, and throughout the hill country of Judea, people were talking about all these things."

MAYA: That word "fear" - phobos - in biblical context often means religious awe rather than terror. Something sacred is happening. Something that exceeds normal categories.

SAMUEL: And then the question that hangs over everything: "What then will this child become?" People sense they're witnessing something significant, but they don't know what.

MAYA: "For the Lord's hand was with him." Luke confirms what the community suspected - this child has a destiny. Divine involvement is evident even now.

SAMUEL: Let's think about what this passage reveals. First, there's a power struggle over naming. The community has expectations. Elizabeth and Zechariah have divine instruction. Who wins?

MAYA: The parents - acting on angelic authority - override social convention. The child won't be Zechariah junior. He'll be John. "YHWH has shown grace." The name itself is a proclamation.

SAMUEL: Second, Zechariah's healing is tied to obedience. As long as he remained silent about the angel's message, he couldn't speak at all. When he confirms the name Gabriel gave - John - his voice returns.

MAYA: There's something there about the relationship between what we say and what we can say. His silence ended when he spoke truth.

SAMUEL: Third, the community is incorporated into the mystery. They celebrate the birth. They witness the naming controversy. They see the miracle of Zechariah's restored speech. And they're left wondering - what does this mean?

MAYA: The story doesn't explain itself immediately. People are left with questions. That's honest. When sacred events happen, we don't always get instant interpretation.

SAMUEL: The next passage - the Benedictus - will provide Zechariah's interpretation. But the community doesn't have access to that yet. They're in the space of wonder and uncertainty.

MAYA: Which is often where we are too. Something significant happens. We sense it matters. But we don't yet know how or why.

SAMUEL: One more observation. The narrative structure creates parallels between John's birth story and Jesus' birth story in chapter two. Miraculous conception. Community celebration. Naming ceremony. Prophetic interpretation.

MAYA: Luke wants us to see these two children as connected. John prepares the way. Jesus fulfills what John prepares. They're part of one story.

SAMUEL: But John comes first. And his birth creates this ripple of awe and questioning throughout the Judean hill country. "What will this child become?"

MAYA: The answer will unfold. John in the wilderness. John baptizing. John pointing to someone greater. But for now, the question hangs there.

SAMUEL: Next time: Zechariah's prophecy - the Benedictus. What he's been holding inside for nine months of silence finally pours out.
