LUKE 1:1-4 - PERICOPE PODCAST (English)
Title: "Why I'm Writing This"
Duration: ~8 minutes
Hosts: Samuel and Maya

---

SAMUEL: Today we're looking at the opening four verses of Luke's Gospel - his prologue. And it's genuinely unique in the New Testament.

MAYA: It's the only passage in Luke that's written in formal classical Greek. The rest of the Gospel has a completely different style. So what's going on?

SAMUEL: Luke is doing something very intentional. He's signaling to educated readers: I know how to write history the way respectable historians write history. This isn't myth or legend. This is carefully researched narrative.

MAYA: Let's read it. "Many people have tried to put together an account of what happened among us - the events passed down by those who saw everything from the beginning and became messengers of the word. So I decided, after looking carefully into it all from the start, to write it out for you in order, most excellent Theophilus, so you can know for certain what you've been taught."

SAMUEL: One sentence in Greek. Four verses in our translation. It's dense with information about Luke's method and purpose.

MAYA: Let's unpack it. First - "Many people have tried." So Luke isn't the first to write about Jesus. He knows other accounts exist.

SAMUEL: Probably Mark's Gospel, probably a collection of Jesus' sayings that scholars call Q. Maybe others we don't have anymore. He's not criticizing them - he's acknowledging that this story matters enough that multiple people have tried to tell it.

MAYA: Then he mentions his sources - "those who saw everything from the beginning and became messengers of the word." Eyewitnesses who became preachers.

SAMUEL: That word "eyewitness" in Greek is autoptes - it's where we get "autopsy." Literally, "one who sees for oneself." Luke is claiming access to people who were actually there.

MAYA: And he's done his homework. "After looking carefully into it all from the start" - the Greek suggests systematic investigation. He's traced things back, interviewed sources, compared accounts.

SAMUEL: Then comes Theophilus. "Most excellent Theophilus." That title - "most excellent" - was used for Roman officials. So this is either a real person of high status who's funding the publication, or it's a symbolic name.

MAYA: Because Theophilus means "lover of God" - from theos, God, and philos, lover or friend. Some scholars think Luke is addressing anyone who loves God.

SAMUEL: Either way, he's writing for someone who's already heard the basics - "what you've been taught." The Greek word there is katecheo, which gives us "catechism." Theophilus has received instruction. Luke is confirming and deepening that knowledge.

MAYA: And his goal: "so you can know for certain." Not blind faith. Not wishful thinking. Confidence based on evidence and careful investigation.

SAMUEL: This is fascinating for modern readers who might assume faith and evidence are opposites. Luke is saying the opposite - I want you to have certainty, and I'm providing historical grounding for that certainty.

MAYA: Now here's where it gets interesting. After these four verses of polished classical Greek, verse five begins completely differently. "In the days of Herod king of Judea..." Suddenly we're reading something that sounds like the Hebrew Bible.

SAMUEL: The Greek style changes dramatically. It becomes what scholars call "Septuagintal" - mimicking the style of the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures. Luke does this on purpose.

MAYA: He's saying: I can write like a Greek historian, but I'm choosing to tell this story in the language of Israel's prophets. The style itself is making a theological claim.

SAMUEL: That what's happening with Jesus isn't just another historical event. It's the continuation - the climax - of Israel's story with God.

MAYA: So what do we do with these four verses today?

SAMUEL: I think they give permission to ask hard questions. Luke didn't expect people to believe without evidence. He compiled evidence. He investigated. He wanted his readers to have grounds for confidence.

MAYA: At the same time, he didn't pretend to be a neutral observer. He calls it "good news." He's committed to what he's reporting. But that doesn't mean he's making things up.

SAMUEL: The best historians often care deeply about their subject. The question is whether they're honest about their sources and methods. Luke is explicit about both.

MAYA: There's also something about the shift in style - from classical Greek to biblical Hebrew patterns. It suggests that sometimes telling the truth requires changing how you speak.

SAMUEL: Academic language is good for some things. Poetic, prophetic language is good for others. Luke uses both because the story he's telling requires both.

MAYA: Four verses. An entire philosophy of how to approach this text. Not bad for an introduction.

SAMUEL: Next time: An old priest, an empty nursery, and an angel in the temple.
